Technical Abstract (Limit 200 words) Antiprotons offer the largest specific energy of any material known to mankind, making them extremely attractive as a space propulsion fuel. Several concepts have been proposed over the past decade. An attractive option uses the antiprotons to create a dense hot plasma which, when exhausted from the confining vessel, provides direct thrust. Another option uses the plasma to trigger nuclear microfusion reactions providing the requisite specific impulse and thrust for interplanetary, and even interstellar, missions. Recent progress by G.A. Smith at Penn State University toward the creation of a portable antiproton trap and a recently funded project at MSFC to develop the next generation trap with substantially greater capacity (10^12 antiprotons), now make it possible to design an Antimatter Plasma Gun (APG) derived around the Penning Trap principles. We foresee designing and eventually building a device which can provide a bright, small-emittance ion beam with several eV to KeV energy. This device would enable the aforementioned space propulsion applications to be investigated in a laboratory setting as well as revolutionize many other applications of plasmas which currently depend on large, expensive, and hazardous sources. 8. Potential Commercial Application(s)(Limit 200 words) The potential for commercial applications of the Antimatter Plasma Gun (APG) is great because the APG would provide a compact and inexpensive alternative to the large and expensive sources of dense, hot plasma that exist today. The potential applications include: advanced space propulsion, fusion energy research, plasma surface coating technology, high-power plasma switches, gas discharge lasers, isotope separation, research into magnetohydrodyamic principles for electric power plants and portable power supplies, pollution control and reduction, and materials processing.
