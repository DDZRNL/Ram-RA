Technical Abstract (Limit 200 words) In the present computer market, premium is placed upon computational performance measures, not on the quality of input current waveform, leading to power factor and power quality problems at computing facilities with no off-the-shelf remedies currently available. Montana State University - Bozeman has developed power conversion technology employing Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) for instanteous control of ac power flow. In the proposed project, PWM ac-ac power conversion technology will be applied to solve power quality problems posed by loads such as data processing and computing equipment. A Unity Power Factor (UPF) interface device, which shapes the supply line current to be sinusoidal, will be developed. The UPF will have an input that connects to the utility outlet and an ouput that supplies power to the computing equipment. The user can plug the UPF interface into a standard wall outlet and plug nonlinear loads such as computers into its outlet. The objective for Phase I of the proposed project is to study the feasibility of the proposed UPF approach for computing equipment. A computer simulation of the proposed UPF interface will be developed and a laboratory breadboard prototype of the interface will be developed to verify the results of the simulations. 8. Potential Commercial Application(s)(Limit 200 words) The proposed research can have a definite commercial impact with respect to several potential commercial markets such as: (1) a replacement for all switch mode power supplies, (2) as an input power accessory where substantial numbers of PC computer devices are operated, and (3) as an accessory to the ballast component where large numbers of flouresent lighting fixtures are used. A particular advantage that successful UPF development can offer over present practices is in buildings and facilities where large numbers of switching mode power supplies are utilized. This is due to the fact that the original wiring and electrical equipment ratings were often established assuming power factors based upon the phase angle and not the actual power factor determined by the switching function. The actual power factor results in electrical currents in excess of the angle based determination.
