Technical Abstract (Limit 200 words) Advanced catalysts with high efficiency and improved physical properties are projected as valuable tools for both spacecraft life support systems and industrial processes. It is well documented that reductions in expendable resources are required for cost-effective presence of man in space. Catalytic oxidation to remove contaminants in air and water is a key unit operation available to achieve this. These processes use fixed bed reactors consisting of supported noble metal catalysts. The two most widely used supports are alumina and activated carbon. According to extensive research by both NASA and industry, an order of magnitude more efficiency is obtained by using carbon versus alumina. Carbon-supported catalysts are susceptible to long-term physical degradation (due to shear forces and metal leaching) and chemical degradation (by oxidation of solid carbon to CO2) under reactor conditions. Materials with markedly improved physical stability, increased temperature range, and high catalytic activity are proposed. Phase I will investigate treatments to improve the operating temperature range and adherence of the catalytic metal to the carbon surface. Screening tests will be performed to assess suitability of new materials for both space and industrial process conditions. Phase II will consist of full development and long-term testing resulting in materials for dual-use. 8. Potential Commercial Application(s)(Limit 200 words) This technology, if successfully developed, has been identified by a major industrial catalyst company as having potential for immediate product sales of 6,000-20,000 lbs catalyst/month (with product sales over 1.5 million dollars/month) for "clean" chemical processing and environmental technology. An additional five industrial processes, each with product sales over 100 million dollars/year have been documented that would also be likely markets for the proposed product.
