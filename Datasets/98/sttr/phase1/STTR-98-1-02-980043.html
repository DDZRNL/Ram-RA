Technical Abstract (Limit 200 words) Physical Sciences Inc. (PSI), in collaboration with the Y-Division (Laser Division) of Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL), proposes to develop space-qualified optical components that are building blocks of the Optical Waveguide (OW) solar plant lighting system. In the OW system, solar radiation is collected by the concentrator which transfers the concentrated solar radiation to the OW transmission line consisting of low-loss optical fibers. The OW line transmits the plant lighting spectra (< 700 nm) to the plant growth chamber where the solar radiation from the optical fibers is de-focused for optimum intensity for plant growing. The non-plant growing spectra (> 700 nm) is utilized for generating electric power which will provide plant lighting when solar radiation is not available. PSI has been developing the OW technology in the past few years. Technologies resident at LLNL that are pertinent to the proposed research program include: optics and material technologies for laser power transmission; large diameter optical fiber technologies for continuous wave (CW) power transmission; thermal effects on materials and structure; vacuum technology; and space environment effects. The two technologies resident at LLNL and PSI can be effectively combined for development of the space- qualified optical components for solar plant lighting system. 8. Potential Commercial Application(s)(Limit 200 words) The Optical Waveguide (OW) solar plant lighting technology is well adapted for growing plants in a remote location or in an enclosed chamber using solar irradiance. The proposed program aims at developing space-qualified optical components for utilization of solar energy in space. In any commercial application areas, whether energy, telecommunication, transportation, or agriculture, innovative concept must be substantiated by enabling component technologies. The space-qualified optical components for solar plant lighting, when developed to technical maturity, will find applications beyond the plant lighting. They will enable more complete utilization of solar energy not only in space but also in many terrestrial applications. Commercial applications of the solar plant lighting system include the use of the system for energy-efficient crop growing in the polar region, with the state of Alaska as a case in point. The government of Alaska is very interested in employing technologies that are designed to harness natural sunlight for growing plants in closed environments. Another important application of the technology is for indoor lighting in highly crowded cities wherein numerous buildings do not receive any solar irradiance.
