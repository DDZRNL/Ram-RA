Technical Abstract (Limit 200 words) ETREMA Products, Inc. and the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) will develop and commercialize new smart material and device products for use in advanced spacecraft and aircraft in order to reduce production and operating costs. Work done at UCLA in the development of magnetostrictive composite materials using epoxies will be extended and transferred to industry. The production of these new smart materials will enable low cost devices that surpass the limitations of current composite material technologies based on epoxies or typical polyimides, including limited service temperature, low fracture toughness, material instability and hydrolysis. The advanced magnetostrictive composite materials and devices are enabled by combining two novel material classes: advanced resin systems and magnetostrictive smart materials. These advanced composite materials will provide a high performance smart material with structural capabilities, excellent durability and high temperature capability, while overcoming the electrical connectivity problems associated with piezoceramic composites. With the commercialization of TERFENOL- D alloys, and devices and systems based thereon, ETREMA has proven it is successful in materials technology transfer. It can be equally successful in the development of low-cost production methods and products based on improved smart materials and transducers (actuators, sensors, etc.). 8. Potential Commercial Application(s)(Limit 200 words) The list of potential products (including those requiring cryogenic performance), which will be made possible by this material development, can be broken down into several categories which include: actuators of all types, pumps, valves, active flow control systems, vibration dampers, structural health monitoring systems, scanner motors, MEMS, thin films, sensors, etc. By taking advantage of the broad thermal capabilities of the novel constituent materials, these products can be designed for use in temperatures ranging from cryogenics (such as NASA's NGST satellite) to over 200øC. Actuators will find use in mirror and other optics positioning, deployment of structures, powering remote vehicle manipulators, locking and latching mechanisms for docking, and other very low frequency applications, or applications which require a position and hold capability with high stiffness. Medium frequency applications will include pumps, valves, and both active and passive structural vibration dampers. High frequency applications could include such things as instrument scanner motors and even the possibility of generating ultrasonic acoustic streaming for moving liquids without pressure buildup and heating.
