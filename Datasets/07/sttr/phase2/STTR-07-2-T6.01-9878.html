TECHNICAL ABSTRACT (Limit 2000 characters, approximately = 200=20 words)
Phase I demonstrated the technical feasibility of = creating=20 surface acoustic wave (SAW) based humidity sensors that respond = rapidly=20 (under 0.5 second) and reversibly to a wide range of humidity = levels using=20 nanoparticle and thin polymer films. This proposal describes a = Phase II=20 effort that will result in the development of multiple uniquely=20 identifiable, wirelessly interrogable, passive humidity sensors, = and a=20 wireless interrogation system to obtain and interpret the sensor=20 responses. The interrogation system used will be a slightly = modified=20 version of the system designed by ASR&D for use in the = wireless SAW=20 concrete maturity monitoring system currently being developed by = ASR&D=20 under funding from the Maryland Technology Development Corporation = (TEDCO)=20 and under the NASA Phase II STTR contract NNK07EA38C. ASR&D = thinks=20 that this interrogation system, which incorporates coded sensor = time=20 diversity with self-synchronization, has significant advantages = over the=20 OFC-sensor-based systems previously developed by the ASR&D and = the=20 University of Central Florida in terms of increased range and the = larger=20 number of sensors that can simultaneously operate without sensor = to sensor=20 interference. The proposed Phase II effort has technical = objectives that=20 aim (i) to further the development and implementation of the = passive=20 wireless SAW humidity sensor technology for which preliminary = technical=20 feasibility was demonstrated in Phase I, (ii) to modify existing=20 breadboard wireless interrogator electronics to operate with these = sensors, and (iii) to produce prototype sensor devices capable of=20 operation with the modified breadboard wireless interrogator = system to=20 produce a multiple sensor system usable for field demonstration of = the=20 technology. Wireless system demonstrations will be conducted using = laboratory samples of the composites used in wind turbines or = other NASA=20 relevant applications. At the completion of the Phase II effort, = the=20 sensors and system will be at TRL 4-5.=20

POTENTIAL NASA COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS (Limit 1500 = characters,=20 approximately 150 words)
The primary NASA application for the = proposed=20 wireless multisensor humidity measurement system would be for = detection of=20 water infiltration in composite structures and insulating foam, a=20 significant problem for a variety of NASA programs. The spray on = foam=20 insulation (SOFI) used on the shuttle external fuel tank can = absorb water.=20 Upon launch, water trapped in the foam can undergo aerodynamic = heating,=20 causing rapid expansion of foam sections as the water turns to = steam. This=20 can lead to delaminations and sections of the foam breaking off = causing=20 other hazards. Similar problems exist for composite = aerostructures. Both=20 honeycomb foam core carbon composites can experience explosive = expansion=20 of water vapor due to aerodynamic heating during launch, if = excessive=20 water infiltration has occurred. This major problem dictates that=20 aerodynamic heating be taken into account when launch windows are=20 determined. Installation of arrays of individually coded passive = wireless=20 humidity sensors inside such structures would allow for = non-destructive=20 determination of water levels internal to the structure, providing = launch=20 personnel with valuable information to optimize the flight path.=20 Application to SOFI and nonconductive composite materials would be = straightforward, while for electrically conductive carbon = composites=20 innovative techniques to transmit the activating signal through = the=20 composite layers would be necessary, such as the = acoustic-electrical=20 transmission demonstrated by JPL.=20

POTENTIAL NON-NASA COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS (Limit 1500=20 characters, approximately 150 words)
Measurement of humidity = is one of=20 the most commonly used metrics in industrial process control, and = along=20 with temperature, is critical to a wide range of industrial and=20 manufacturing processes. Numerous commercially available humidity = sensors=20 are used in the manufacture of household appliances, automobiles, = HVAC=20 equipment, printing equipment, telecommunication equipment, = computation=20 equipment, and a wide range of industrial machinery. None of these = commercial sensors, however, are passive, able to operate = wirelessly, and=20 capable of operation at extreme temperatures. Small, rugged, = inexpensive,=20 reliable sensors that can rapidly sense a range of humidity levels = under=20 extreme environments would prove beneficial to a range of = applications.=20 Additionally, the rapid response of the sensors demonstrated in = Phase I=20 could make these devices advantageous for certain applications = where=20 wireless operation is not required. The two most promising short = term=20 applications are detection of water intrusion in composite = structures for=20 wind turbines and the aerospace industry, and monitoring of joint = sleeves=20 in oil and gas pipelines for corrosion-inducing humidity levels = and/or=20 condensing water. Additional applications for which ASR&D has = received=20 inquiries from potential end users and industrial collaborators = include=20 monitoring for water intrusion in commercial built up roofing, = humidity=20 detection surrounding high-value consumer windows and doors, and = water=20 intrusion in concrete structures.=20
